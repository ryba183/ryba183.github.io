Cloud Microphysics

Cloud microphysics is a field that studies the processes occurring within clouds, particularly the formation and evolution of cloud droplets. Warm rain creation is a key aspect of cloud microphysics, where droplets grow through collision and coalescence to form precipitation without the involvement of ice particles. However, a challenge in warm rain formation is the size gap problem. It refers to the difficulty of explaining the rapid growth of tiny cloud droplets into raindrops through collision alone, as the efficiency of collisions decreases with increasing droplet sizes. Simulating the collision between cloud droplets is a complex task due to various factors involved. It requires considering droplet properties such as size, velocity, and concentration, as well as environmental factors like turbulence and electric charges. Achieving accurate modeling and simulation of the collision process is challenging due to factors such as droplet concentration, size distribution, and ambient conditions.

University of Warsaw Lagrangian Cloud Model

  • Anelistic Large Eddy Simulation (LES);
  • 1D, 2D or 3D simulations;
  • The Multidimensional Positive-Definite Advection Transport Algorithm (MPDATA) is used to model transport of Eulerian variables;
  • SGS turbulence modeled with Smagorinsky scheme or implicit LES;
  • Models for sub-grid scale (SGS) motion, condensation, and coalescence of Super-Droplets;
  • Super-Droplet Method (SDM):
    • The Super-Droplets (SD) are computational objects used in Lagrangian particle-based microphysics methods;
    • Each SD represents a large number of identical real particles. SD are described by its: position, dry and/or wet radius and multiplicity;
    • Super-Droplets microphysics can be calculated using GPUs;

Modeling Collision-Coalescence in Particle Microphysics

A fundamental component of the Super-Droplet Method is the All Or Nothing (AON) algorithm, which governs the behavior of SDs following collisions between two SDs. The AON algorithm ensures mass conservation during these collision events. Let's consider two SDs, SD₁ and SD₂, with respective properties of radius (r₁ and r₂) and multiplicity (ζ₁ and ζ₂). When ζ₁ exceeds ζ₂, the post-collision properties of SD₁ and SD₂ can be described as follows: ζ₁ decreases by ζ₂, while r₁ remains unchanged. In the case of SD₂, ζ₂ remains unchanged, but r₂ increases in accordance with mass conservation. To calculate the resulting radius, we sum up the cubed radii of r₁ and r₂ and then take the cubic root.

A fundamental component of the Super-Droplet Method is the All Or Nothing (AON) algorithm, which governs the behavior of SDs following collisions between two SDs. The AON algorithm ensures mass conservation during these collision events. Let's consider two SDs, SD₁ and SD₂, with respective properties of radius (r₁ and r₂) and multiplicity (ζ₁ and ζ₂). When ζ₁ exceeds ζ₂, the post-collision properties of SD₁ and SD₂ can be described as follows: ζ₁ decreases by ζ₂, while r₁ remains unchanged. In the case of SD₂, ζ₂ remains unchanged, but r₂ increases in accordance with mass conservation. To calculate the resulting radius, we sum up the cubed radii of r₁ and r₂ and then take the cubic root. In my research, I conducted sensitivity tests on the All Or Nothing algorithm within the context of Cumulus Congestus Cloud, drawing from the Lasher-Trap et al. (2005) setup. Specifically, I investigated the influence of the number of SDs per grid box, SD initialization method, and coalescence substep on the behavior of the AON algorithm. Additionally, to further advance my research, I explored the behavior of the AON algorithm under varying precipitation conditions, including scenarios with minimal rainfall ('LR'), moderate rainfall ('MR'), and heavy precipitation ('HR'). In these simulations, the velocity field remains consistent across all scenarios, with differences only in the microphysics aspects of the simulations.
